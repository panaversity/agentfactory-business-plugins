# HARD QUESTIONS PREP — AP Automation Startup

**Context:** 8 customers, $16K MRR, 3% monthly churn, no technical co-founder, outsourced development, pitching angel investors.

---

## THE 15 HARDEST QUESTIONS

### 1. "Why don't you have a technical co-founder?"

**Why they ask:** This is your known weakness. They want to know if you understand how much risk this creates.

**Honest answer:** "I haven't found the right person yet, and I'd rather outsource with discipline than bring on a wrong-fit CTO. I have [X dev shop/team] building with clear specs, and part of this raise is to hire a VP Engineering or CTO. I've been able to get to $16K MRR and 8 paying customers managing the outsourced team — that's execution evidence, not a gap I'm ignoring."

**Before the pitch:** Have a specific CTO hiring plan. Timeline, compensation (equity + cash), where you're sourcing candidates, what "great" looks like. If you have any candidates in conversation, mention them. Investors need to believe this gets solved in 90 days post-close.

---

### 2. "What happens if your dev team disappears tomorrow?"

**Why they ask:** Outsourced development = key-person risk on a team you don't employ.

**Honest answer:** State specifically: Do you own all the code and IP? Is it well-documented? Could another team pick it up? If yes, say so with specifics. If there's concentration risk on one developer, acknowledge it.

**Before the pitch:** Confirm you have full IP ownership in writing. Have your codebase in your own repos. Know how long onboarding a new team would take.

---

### 3. "3% monthly churn means you lose a third of your customers every year. How do you fix that?"

**Why they ask:** 3% monthly = ~31% annual churn. That's not catastrophic for early-stage SMB SaaS, but it's not good. They want to know if you understand the math and have a plan.

**Honest answer:** "3% monthly is where we are today with 8 customers — small sample, so one churned customer moves the number significantly. [Explain why the customers who churned left — was it product, fit, or budget?] Our retention improves as we move toward [ideal customer profile] because [specific reason]. Our target is under 2% monthly by [date], and here's what we're doing to get there: [specific retention initiatives]."

**Before the pitch:** Know exactly which customers churned, why, and what you changed. Have a cohort view if possible — are newer customers retaining better?

---

### 4. "Who are your 8 customers and how did you get them?"

**Why they ask:** They want to understand your sales motion, whether these are real paying customers, and whether the acquisition channel is repeatable.

**Honest answer:** Be specific. Company sizes, industries, how you found them (cold outreach, referral, inbound), how long the sales cycle was, what they pay. If 6 of 8 came from your personal network, say so — but explain what the scalable channel will be.

**Before the pitch:** Have a one-liner on each customer. Know your average deal size, sales cycle length, and which channel produced each customer.

---

### 5. "Bill.com is public, Tipalti raised $500M, Airbase got acquired by Paylocity. Why would anyone use you instead?"

**Why they ask:** AP automation is a crowded space. They need to understand your wedge.

**Honest answer:** You need a specific, defensible answer. Who do you serve that they don't? What do you do differently? Is it a segment (small manufacturers, nonprofits, construction companies), a workflow (specific ERP integration, specific approval flow), or a pricing model? "We're cheaper" is weak. "We're the only AP tool that integrates natively with [specific ERP] used by [specific segment]" is strong.

**Before the pitch:** Have a competitive matrix. Know Bill.com's pricing, Tipalti's ICP, and where the gaps are. Your 8 customers chose you over something — know what that something was.

---

### 6. "What's your LTV:CAC?"

**Why they ask:** This tells them if you have a real business or are buying revenue.

**Honest answer:** With $16K MRR across 8 customers, your average revenue per customer is $2,000/month. At 3% monthly churn, average customer lifetime is ~33 months. So LTV ≈ $66,000. Your CAC depends on what you spend to acquire each customer. If you've spent very little (founder-led sales, no paid marketing), your LTV:CAC is very high — say so. But be honest that you haven't tested paid acquisition yet.

**Before the pitch:** Calculate this precisely. Know the number. If you don't know your CAC, estimate it honestly including your time.

---

### 7. "How much are you raising and what exactly does it buy?"

**Why they ask:** They want to know if you've thought through use of funds and whether the amount gets you to a meaningful milestone.

**Honest answer:** State the number, the instrument (SAFE? convertible note? priced round?), and the allocation: X% engineering/CTO hire, Y% sales, Z% runway. Most importantly: "This capital takes us from $16K MRR to $[target] MRR in 18 months, which positions us to raise a seed round at $[valuation]." The milestone must be specific and credible.

**Before the pitch:** Model your burn rate post-raise. Know exactly how many months of runway the raise provides. Have a base case and conservative case.

---

### 8. "You're a solo non-technical founder in a technical space. How are you making product decisions?"

**Why they ask:** They're probing whether you actually understand your product deeply or are just relaying customer requests to developers.

**Honest answer:** Explain your product process. How do you decide what to build? How do you prioritize? How do you QA? Do you use the product yourself? Can you demo it cold? Your 8 customers are your credibility here — you built something people pay for. Lean into that.

**Before the pitch:** Be able to demo your product flawlessly. Know every feature, every limitation, every workaround. Nothing kills confidence faster than a non-technical founder who can't navigate their own product.

---

### 9. "What's your gross margin?"

**Why they ask:** If you're outsourcing development, your cost structure might be ugly. They want to know if this is a SaaS-margin business (70-85%) or something worse.

**Honest answer:** Calculate it. Revenue minus hosting, infrastructure, payment processing, and any variable costs tied to serving customers. Development costs for new features are typically not included in COGS, but ongoing maintenance and support might be. If your margins are strong, lead with the number. If they're weak because of outsourcing costs, explain what changes post-CTO hire.

**Before the pitch:** Know your gross margin to the percentage point. Have a view of what it looks like at $50K MRR and $100K MRR.

---

### 10. "What does your pipeline look like? Where does $16K MRR go in 6 months without this capital?"

**Why they ask:** They want to know if you're growing organically or stalling. If you need their money to survive, that's a weaker position than if you need it to accelerate.

**Honest answer:** Be specific. "I have X prospects in active conversations, Y in trial, Z with proposals out. Without capital, I project reaching $[X]K MRR in 6 months because [reason]. With capital, I project $[Y]K because [specific acceleration — hire, marketing spend, product capability]."

**Before the pitch:** Have a real pipeline. Even if it's 5 prospects, know where each one stands.

---

### 11. "Why are you raising now and not 6 months from now when you'd have more traction?"

**Why they ask:** They want to understand your urgency. If you're running out of money, that's a red flag. If there's a market window, that's compelling.

**Honest answer:** The best answer is: "I could wait, but [specific reason why now is optimal — competitive window, customer demand outpacing capacity, CTO candidate ready to start, enterprise deal that needs features we can't build yet]." The worst answer is: "I'm running out of personal savings."

**Before the pitch:** Have a genuine reason beyond cash need. What opportunity do you miss by waiting?

---

### 12. "Walk me through your unit economics. What does it cost to serve one customer?"

**Why they ask:** They want to know if each incremental customer is profitable and at what scale the business becomes self-sustaining.

**Honest answer:** Hosting cost per customer, support time per customer, onboarding cost, any integration costs. With 8 customers, you should know this precisely. If some customers are more profitable than others, say so — it shows you understand your ICP.

**Before the pitch:** Build a simple unit economics table. Revenue per customer, cost to serve, cost to acquire, gross profit per customer, payback period.

---

### 13. "What's your moat? What stops Bill.com from adding your feature tomorrow?"

**Why they ask:** Features are not moats. They want to know what's defensible.

**Honest answer:** At your stage, honest moats are: deep integration with a specific ecosystem that's painful to replicate, proprietary data from customer usage, switching costs (once AP is automated, nobody wants to switch), and speed of iteration in a niche that large players ignore. Don't claim a moat you don't have. "We're building switching costs through deep ERP integration and workflow customization" is honest and believable.

**Before the pitch:** Be able to explain why Bill.com or Tipalti won't serve your specific customers well even if they tried. Segment specificity is your friend.

---

### 14. "What's your valuation expectation, and how did you arrive at it?"

**Why they ask:** They want to know if you're reasonable. At $16K MRR ($192K ARR), most angels will expect a $2-5M valuation range depending on growth rate, market, and team.

**Honest answer:** If using a SAFE, state the cap. Justify it with comparable raises in your space, your growth rate, and your ARR. Don't over-anchor. "We're raising on a SAFE with a $[X]M cap, which reflects [X]x ARR multiple, consistent with [comparable companies at this stage]." Be prepared to negotiate, but have a floor.

**Before the pitch:** Research comparable angel/pre-seed rounds in AP automation or B2B SaaS at your revenue level. Know what multiples are reasonable.

---

### 15. "If this doesn't work, what do you do?"

**Why they ask:** They want to assess your commitment and whether you've thought about downside scenarios. They also want to know if there's any salvage value.

**Honest answer:** "If the current approach doesn't work, here's what I'd pivot to based on what we've learned from our 8 customers: [specific pivot option]. But I want to be clear — I've quit [previous job/commitment] to do this, I have paying customers and growing revenue, and my focus is making this work, not planning failure." Conviction matters, but so does intellectual honesty.

**Before the pitch:** Have a genuine answer. Not a rehearsed deflection, but evidence that you've thought about it maturely.
