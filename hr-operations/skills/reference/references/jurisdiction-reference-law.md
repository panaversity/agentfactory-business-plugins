# Jurisdiction-Specific Reference Law

## United Kingdom

**Key case**: Spring v Guardian Assurance [1995] 2 AC 296

- Established that employers owe a **duty of care** when providing references
- A reference must be **true, accurate, and fair** — not misleading by omission
- Employer can be liable in negligence for a reference that is inaccurate and causes loss
- No legal obligation to provide a reference (except in regulated sectors — e.g., financial services under FCA rules)
- **Safe practice**: Factual references (dates, title, rehire eligibility) carry minimal risk. Opinion references require care.

**Regulated sectors**:

- Financial services: FCA requires regulatory references for certain roles (SM&CR regime)
- Education: Statutory requirement to provide references for safeguarding purposes
- Healthcare: Employers expected to provide references; NHS has standard templates

**Data protection**: References are personal data under UK GDPR. Subject access requests can include references given (but exemptions may apply to references received).

## United States

**General principle**: No federal law requires employers to provide references

- **Qualified privilege**: Most states provide qualified privilege for good-faith reference information — protects employers from defamation claims if information is truthful and not malicious
- **State variation is significant**:
  - Some states have reference immunity statutes (e.g., Florida, Georgia, Ohio)
  - Some states restrict what can be disclosed without employee consent
  - California: employer may disclose job performance and qualifications to prospective employers (Labor Code s.1053)
- **Safe practice**: Many US employers adopt "name, rank, and dates" policies to minimise risk
- **Negligent referral**: Employer may face liability for failing to disclose known dangerous behaviour (Randi W. v Muroc Joint Unified School District, 1997)

## Pakistan

**No statutory framework** for employment references

- References are governed by **contract and custom**, not statute
- Common practice: employer provides an "experience certificate" confirming employment dates, role, and basic performance
- No qualified privilege statute — defamation claims follow general tort law
- **Safe practice**: Factual certificates are standard and expected; opinion references are uncommon and carry unquantified risk

## United Arab Emirates

**Federal Decree-Law No. 33 of 2021 (Labour Law)**

- **Article 13 (end-of-service certificate)**: Employer is **obligated** to provide a certificate of service on termination, including dates of employment, job title, and last salary — at no charge to the employee
- Employer may not include anything in the certificate that could harm the employee's reputation or reduce their chances of finding new employment
- **Visa cancellation**: Employment cancellation and visa status are linked — reference timing matters for the employee's immigration status
- **Safe practice**: Comply with Article 13 requirements; keep additional references factual; do not include negative commentary that could be challenged under the Labour Law

## General Guidance

When jurisdiction is not specified:

1. Default to **factual reference** (dates, title, employment confirmation)
2. Do not include performance commentary unless explicitly authorised and verifiable
3. Do not include salary unless employee has given written consent
4. Always mark output for HR review before sending
5. Flag any jurisdiction-specific obligations (e.g., UAE mandatory certificate, UK FCA regulatory reference)
