## PHASE 2: KEY KNOWLEDGE AREAS BY TEAM MEMBER

### 1. CFO — Sarah (Risk: HIGH — 15/15)

EMPLOYEE: [CFO] | ROLE: Chief Financial Officer | TENURE: 12 years | RISK: HIGH

KEY KNOWLEDGE AREAS (ranked by risk):

1. **Board and investor relationships** — 12 years of relationship history; understands each board member's priorities, communication preferences, and tolerance for risk. This knowledge is entirely tacit and relational. No one else attends board meetings or holds these relationships.

2. **Banking and lending relationships** — Negotiated all current credit facilities; knows the bank's internal risk appetite, key contacts beyond the relationship manager, and the informal commitments made during negotiations. Loss of this knowledge could affect covenant renegotiations and future facility terms.

3. **Strategic financial planning methodology** — The long-range financial model, scenario assumptions, and strategic trade-offs live primarily in the CFO's head. The spreadsheets exist but the reasoning behind key assumptions and the mental model for strategic decisions are undocumented.

4. **Organisational history and institutional context** — 12 years of "what we tried that didn't work," why certain policies exist, who the key internal stakeholders are and how to navigate them, and the political context behind past decisions. This is the deepest form of tacit knowledge and the hardest to capture.

5. **Regulatory and audit strategy** — Approach to managing external auditors, tax strategy rationale, relationships with tax advisors, and understanding of the organisation's risk exposure history.

6. **Vendor and insurance broker relationships** — Long-standing relationships with key financial service providers, knowledge of negotiated terms, renewal cycles, and service level expectations.

### 2. Financial Controller — (Risk: HIGH — 12/15)

EMPLOYEE: [Financial Controller] | ROLE: Financial Controller | TENURE: 6 years | RISK: HIGH

KEY KNOWLEDGE AREAS (ranked by risk):

1. **Internal controls framework** — Designed and maintains the current control environment. Knows which controls are formally documented, which are informal practices, and critically, which controls exist because of past failures. No one else understands the full control map.

2. **Month-end and year-end close process** — The documented close checklist exists, but the real process includes undocumented workarounds, exception handling, and sequencing dependencies that only the Controller knows. "If I followed our documented process exactly, what would I get wrong?" — this question would yield significant value here.

3. **External audit management** — Sole relationship holder with the audit team. Knows the auditors' focus areas, the requests that will come, how to pre-empt issues, and the informal expectations beyond the engagement letter.

4. **Chart of accounts rationale and GL structure** — Understands why accounts are structured as they are, which accounts have historical baggage, and how the GL maps to reporting requirements. Changes made without this context risk breaking reporting integrity.

5. **Intercompany and consolidation processes** — If applicable, holds knowledge of elimination entries, transfer pricing methodology, and cross-entity reconciliation procedures that are partially documented at best.

CAPTURE METHOD:
- Interview-based (for controls rationale, audit management, institutional context)
- Shadowing / observation (for month-end close — watch the actual process vs. documentation)
- Pair working (for GL structure and consolidation — work alongside successor candidates)

SESSIONS RECOMMENDED:
4 sessions x 90 minutes = 6 hours total (spread over 6-8 weeks)

|---------|---------------|--------|------------------|
| 1 | Internal controls — design rationale & informal controls | Interview | Week 1-2 |
| 2 | Month-end / year-end close — reality vs. documentation | Shadowing + interview (schedule at month-end) | Week 3-4 |
| 3 | External audit management & GL structure | Interview | Week 5-6 |
| 4 | Consolidation processes & gap review | Pair working + review of Sessions 1-3 articles | Week 7-8 |

OUTPUTS:

CFO and Controller sessions can run in parallel (different interviewers or alternating weeks).

### Interview Guide — Proactive Framing

Since no one is departing, the opening framing must be adjusted:

**OPENING (5 minutes):**
"We're running our annual knowledge audit across the finance team. The goal is to make sure the critical knowledge and insights you've built up over [N] years here are properly documented and shared — not because anyone is going anywhere, but because it makes the whole team more resilient. Think of it as insurance. There are no wrong answers — we want what's in your head, not what's in the documents."

**CORE QUESTIONS — apply per knowledge area as defined in the skill's Phase 4 interview guide:**

FOR CLIENT / STAKEHOLDER KNOWLEDGE (CFO — board, investors, banks):
- "Tell me about [stakeholder]. Who are the real decision-makers — not just the named contacts but the people whose opinion actually counts?"
- "What has gone wrong in this relationship and how was it recovered? What would you do differently?"
- "What are the unwritten rules? Things that aren't in any document but that the next person needs to know?"
- "What does [stakeholder] actually care about most? Not what the contract says — what makes them feel it's working?"

FOR PROCESS / METHODOLOGY KNOWLEDGE (Controller — close process, controls; AP/AR Mgr — payment processes):
- "Our process says [X]. Where does reality differ from the documentation?"
- "What are the early warning signs that something is going wrong? The signals you've learned to watch for?"
- "What decisions do you make yourself vs. escalate? Walk me through a recent example."
- "If I followed our documented process exactly, what would I get wrong?"

FOR ORGANISATIONAL / CONTEXT KNOWLEDGE (all — especially CFO):
- "What would you tell someone starting your role tomorrow that is not in any document?"
- "What has this organisation tried that didn't work, and why? What should we not try again?"
- "Who are the internal stakeholders I need to manage carefully? What do I need to know about each of them?"
- "What are you most worried we'll get wrong if this knowledge isn't captured?"
- "If you were planning the next 12 months for your function, what would you prioritise that we haven't done yet?"

**CLOSING:**
"Is there anything we haven't talked about that you think is important for the team to know?"

## RECOMMENDED ACTIONS AND TIMELINE

### Immediate Actions (Weeks 1-2)

1. **Schedule CFO Session 1** (Board & investor relationships) — this is the single highest-risk knowledge domain in the team
2. **Schedule Controller Session 1** (Internal controls rationale) — second highest-risk domain
3. **Assign interviewers** — ideally the Controller interviews the CFO (they have the financial context to ask good follow-up questions) and a senior HR representative interviews the Controller
4. **Confirm knowledge article storage location** — designate a secure, access-controlled repository for CONFIDENTIAL knowledge articles

### Short-Term Actions (Weeks 3-8)

5. **Complete all Controller sessions** (4 sessions by Week 8)
6. **Complete AP/AR Manager sessions** (2 sessions by Week 6)
7. **Begin cross-training programme** — Controller sits in on CFO's banking meetings; AP/AR Manager begins shadowing Controller on month-end close
8. **Review and approve completed knowledge articles** — all articles must be reviewed before moving from DRAFT to approved status

### Medium-Term Actions (Weeks 9-14)

9. **Complete all CFO sessions** (6 sessions by Week 12)
10. **Review all 12 knowledge articles** — assign a reviewer for each article (the person is NOT the author)
11. **Identify remaining gaps** — after all sessions, run a gap analysis: what knowledge domains still have no redundancy?
12. **Create a succession development plan** — based on findings, identify which team members should develop capability in which knowledge domains

### Ongoing Actions (Quarterly)

13. **Quarterly knowledge audit check-in** — review the risk map, update scores, identify new knowledge concentrations
14. **Annual full reassessment** — repeat this full five-factor risk assessment annually
15. **Integrate with onboarding** — new team members should read relevant knowledge articles in their first month

## MATURITY ASSESSMENT

This audit moves the finance team's knowledge management maturity from:

**APQC Level 1 (Initiate)** — ad hoc; no formal KM processes; knowledge loss happens silently

to:

**APQC Level 2-3 (Develop/Standardise)** — structured annual knowledge audits; standardised article format; identified ownership; proactive capture for HIGH-risk holders.

### To reach Level 4 (Optimise):

- Embed knowledge capture triggers in the HRIS (automatic scheduling on role changes, tenure milestones, or departure signals)
- Track article usage metrics (are people reading and using the captured knowledge?)
- Implement continuous improvement on the capture process itself
