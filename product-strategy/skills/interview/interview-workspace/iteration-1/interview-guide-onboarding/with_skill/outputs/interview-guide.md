INTERVIEW GUIDE: Team Onboarding Practices at Mid-Market Companies
================================================================
Purpose:     Understand how mid-market companies currently handle
             onboarding for new team members — what works, what
             breaks, and where the real pain lives — before
             evaluating whether to build a team onboarding feature.
Participants:People managers and HR/People Ops leads at companies
             with 50-500 employees who have onboarded at least 2
             new hires in the past 6 months; n=8-10 target
Duration:    45 minutes
Interviewer: [Name]
Note-taker:  [Name or "interviewer only"]

---

SEGMENT 1: OPENING (5 minutes)
Purpose: build rapport; set expectations; get consent

"Thanks for taking the time to speak with me today. I'm [name],
a product manager at [company]. I'm here to learn from you —
there are no right or wrong answers. I'm interested in how
you bring new people onto your team, not in testing you on
anything. Is it okay if I take notes / record this conversation?
[Get consent]
Before we start — any questions for me?"

---

SEGMENT 2: WARM-UP (5-10 minutes)
Purpose: understand who this person is and how they work

Pick 2-3:

- "Can you tell me a bit about your role and what a typical
  day looks like for you?"
- "How big is your team right now, and how often do you bring
  new people on?"
- "How does onboarding fit into your responsibilities — is it
  something you own, or is it shared with HR or other teams?"

---

SEGMENT 3: CORE DISCOVERY (20-25 minutes)
Purpose: understand the onboarding problem area in depth

Opening (anchor to a specific, real experience):
"I'd like to understand how you currently onboard new team
members. Can you walk me through the last time you brought
someone new onto your team — from the moment they accepted
the offer to the point where they were fully productive?
As concretely as possible."

Depth questions (follow the story):

- "What prompted that hire? What were you hoping they'd
  take off your plate?"
- "What happened on their first day? First week?"
- "What was the hardest part of getting them up to speed?"
- "How did you know when they were 'ready' — fully
  productive? How long did that take?"
- "What tools or documents did you use during that process?"
- "Was there a moment where something fell through the cracks?
  What happened?"
- "If that step didn't exist, what would change for you?"

Workaround probe (always ask):
"Before you had your current process — or if you think back
to the very first time you onboarded someone here — how did
you handle it? [Wait] And do you still use any part of that
earlier approach today?"

Frequency and importance probe:
"How often do you go through this onboarding process?
On a scale from 'frustrating' to 'fine', where does your
current approach sit for you?"

Second story (if time allows):
"You mentioned [pain point from first story]. Was that a
one-time thing, or does that happen regularly? Can you give
me another example?"

---

SEGMENT 4: WRAP-UP (5 minutes)
Purpose: catch anything missed; leave door open

- "Is there anything about onboarding that we haven't talked
  about that you think would be important for me to understand?"
- "If you could change one thing about how onboarding works
  at your company, what would it be?"
- "Is there anyone else you think I should talk to who has a
  different perspective on this — maybe someone in a different
  department or role?"

Close:
"This has been really valuable. I may have some follow-up
questions as I synthesise everything — would it be okay to
reach back out? Thank you so much for your time."

---

INTERVIEWER REMINDERS
- Ask about what they DID, not what they THINK or WANT
  (Behaviour over opinion)
- Ask about the PAST, not the future
  (Past over hypothetical)
- Do NOT mention the onboarding feature idea until after
  Core Discovery is complete
  (Problem before solution)
- When the participant pauses, wait at least 5 seconds
  before speaking
  (Silence is data)
- When something interesting surfaces, ask "why?" — then
  ask "why?" again. Go at least 3 levels deep.
  ("Why" five times)
