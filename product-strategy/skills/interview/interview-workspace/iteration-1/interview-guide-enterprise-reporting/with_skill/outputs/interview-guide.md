INTERVIEW GUIDE: Enterprise Custom Reporting Needs
================================================================
Purpose:     Understand why enterprise customers (500+ employees)
             keep requesting custom reporting — what business
             problems drive the request, what workarounds they
             use today, and what "good enough" would look like —
             before deciding how to invest in reporting features.
Participants:CFOs and Finance Directors at companies with 500+
             employees who have requested custom reporting or
             expressed dissatisfaction with current reporting
             capabilities; n=6-8 target
Duration:    30 minutes (executive-appropriate)
Interviewer: [Name]
Note-taker:  [Name — strongly recommended for executive interviews]

---

SEGMENT 1: OPENING (3 minutes)
Purpose: build rapport; set expectations; get consent

"Thank you for making time today — I know your schedule is
demanding, so I'll keep this focused. I'm [name], a product
manager at [company]. I'm trying to understand how reporting
fits into your work and where we can do better. There are no
right or wrong answers — I'm here to learn from your
experience, not to sell you anything. Is it okay if I take
notes? [Get consent]
Any questions before we begin?"

---

SEGMENT 2: WARM-UP (3-5 minutes)
Purpose: understand this person's world and how reporting fits

Pick 2:

- "Can you give me a quick picture of your role — what does
  a typical month-end or quarter-end look like for you?"
- "How does reporting fit into your day-to-day decision-making?
  What kinds of reports do you rely on most?"
- "How has your reporting needs changed as your organisation
  has grown?"

---

SEGMENT 3: CORE DISCOVERY (18-20 minutes)
Purpose: understand the reporting pain in depth

Opening (anchor to a specific, real experience):
"I'd like to understand a specific situation. Can you walk me
through the last time you needed a report that our product
couldn't give you out of the box — what were you trying to
understand, and what did you end up doing?"

Depth questions (follow the story):

- "What prompted that need? Was it a board request, an audit,
  an internal decision?"
- "What happened next — who did you go to?"
- "How long did it take to get what you needed?"
- "What was the cost of that delay — to you, your team, the
  decision?"
- "How often does this kind of situation come up?"
- "When you say 'custom reporting', what does that actually
  mean in your context — is it different data, different
  format, different frequency, or something else?"

Workaround probe (always ask):
"Before you started asking us for custom reports — or before
you used our product — how did your team handle this? [Wait]
And are you still using any part of that workaround today?"

Budget and decision-making probe:
- "When you evaluate reporting tools, who else is involved
  in that decision?"
- "Have you allocated budget specifically for reporting or
  analytics tooling in the past year?"
- "What would it take for a reporting solution to replace
  the spreadsheets and workarounds your team uses today?"

---

SEGMENT 4: WRAP-UP (4 minutes)
Purpose: catch anything missed; leave door open

- "Is there anything about reporting at your organisation
  that we haven't covered that you think is important for
  me to understand?"
- "If you could change one thing about how you get data
  and reports today, what would it be?"
- "Is there someone on your team — maybe a financial analyst
  or controller — who lives in the reports day-to-day and
  might have a different perspective?"

Close:
"This has been really valuable. I may have some follow-up
questions as I synthesise everything — would it be okay to
reach back out? Thank you so much for your time."

---

INTERVIEWER REMINDERS
- Ask about what they DID, not what they THINK or WANT
  (Behaviour over opinion)
- Ask about the PAST, not the future
  (Past over hypothetical)
- Do NOT mention specific feature ideas (dashboards, APIs,
  self-serve tools) until after Core Discovery is complete
  (Problem before solution)
- When the executive pauses, wait at least 5 seconds —
  senior leaders often need a moment to frame their answer
  (Silence is data)
- When they mention a pain point, ask "why is that a
  problem?" — then "why?" again. Peel back to the
  business impact.
  ("Why" five times)
